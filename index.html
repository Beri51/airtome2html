Wolken teils stark ausgebreite [...]

Wolken teils stark ausgebreitet. Bericht vom Wolkenflug fÃ¼r Reddit: I was flying xc with a friend on a day with good but spreadout cumulus clouds. At one point I tried to wait for him at cloud base because the lift was not that strong. Well, it was, I just hadn't hit the core until now ... 1m/s became 5m/s and I saw white. For some reason, I managed to remain pretty calm. Elevated heart rate, but not panicky. I knew there were no signs of overdevelopment or crowded airspace, and the cloud didn't touch the ground at any point. But I also knew it was spreadout to the point of clouds even connecting to each other at some spots, so it was going to take some time. First odd thing I noticed after being sucked in was the awkward feeling of the canopy being behind me, my feet at head level (cocoon harness) as if I was in some kind of deep stall. I figured the glider was doing fine, it's just a big pendulum after all. The feeling must have been caused by the lack of any visual reference. I closed my eyes for some seconds to "reset" that and imagined myself being right below the canopy - it worked! The feeling was gone. The same situation with a hang glider or sailplane where the angle of attack doesn't auto correct would probably have scared me to death, though. Then, I tried to get out. I decided to not use big ears or spiral, since the lift was too strong for that, and I wanted to keep the glider inflated and control its movements. So I just marked a direction on my instruments and flew straight ahead, leaving the cloud at its side. Except I didn't. With the lack of visual reference again, I was unable to compensate the tiny break inputs you do to keep the glider inflated and ended up doing big circles. The instruments had a delay of one second or so, which made it hard to use them to fly straight. Figuring out how to do that probably took the better part of the 5 cloud minutes. Eventually I saw the ground and happily spiraled down to a more comfortable altitude and kept a healthy distance between myself and cloud base for the rest of the day. I had no real perception of time while being inside, I had to measure it from the igc file later. Contrary to what I had expected, the inside was dry all the time, no water dripping from the risers. But I guess that's not always the case. Lessons learned: Moderate lift below a cloud usually means you just haven't found the core yet, especially on a good day Don't wait at cloud base unless you know what you are doing You lose perception of pitch inside a cloud, but you can rely on the pendulum effect and "reset" that feeling. It's hard to fly straight without a visual reference Instruments' display delay can be problematic, but managable Get an analogue compass for your cockpit Don't fly into the cloud at all, obviously. But don't panic if you do. Do panic if you fly a hang glider.
